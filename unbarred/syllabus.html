This course aims to introduce students to digital humanities tools and methods through readings, discussions, hands-on workshops and collaborative projects. Students will explore a range of ways to critically evaluate and incorporate digital technologies into academic research and learn to build simple websites, apps, maps, games, and data visualizations. Students will code in HTML, CSS, Python, SQL, Jekyll and other programming languages. This hands-on approach aims to empower students by allowing them to "own" the computers they use through becoming familiar with the computers' back ends, and at the same time gain technical skills that will help them get jobs.

We will do computing work under a set of significant constraints of hardware, software and network capacity common to U.S. jails, prisons and detention centers. We are mindful that these constraints also exist in many places around the globe and we believe that the work we do in prison will be useful for other communities with similar constraints. We therefore approach computing with the wish to care for the environment, further social justice and balance gains and costs.


Learning Objectives

Students will:
  • become acquainted with the current landscape of the field of digital humanities
  • become conversant with a range of debates in the field of digital humanities through readings and discussions
  • create websites to host their own digital portfolios
  • create other artifacts that may include apps, maps, games, and data visualizations
  • code
  • create a proposal for a digital project
  • work in teams to develop a small number of the digital projects proposed

Requirements and Structure

Students in the course should complete the following work during the semester:

Reading and Discussion (Weekly)

Students should complete all weekly readings in advance of the class meeting and should take an active part in class discussions

Students are responsible for writing five blog posts on our shared course blog, two short responses to our weekly readings or in-class discussions and one post about a tutorial session they have attended, with the goal of helping other students understand what they learned and/or what they found valuable about the tutorial session

  • three posts about praxis assignments
  • three posts giving feedback to other students' posts about their praxis assignments
  • one post about the project proposal
  • one post about the final project

Tutoring sessions: students are responsible for attending a 30-minute tutoring session once a week. You are free to go to as many tutoring sessions as you like pending space limitations.

Final projects (Due May 20)

Grading

Regular participation in discussions across the range of our face-to-face and online course spaces is essential.

  • Final project (50%)
