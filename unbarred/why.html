More than two million people in prisons, jails and detention centers in the United States do not have access to the internet or to the rapidly changing technologies that increasingly dominate the social and professional landscapes of the world outside. This digital divide constitutes yet another punitive effect of incarceration, because when men and women reenter communities and need to compete in a job market that privileges digital skills, they find themselves at a disadvantage. What office job can a person get when they have not used a computer for years? What jobs can a person without digital skills get? And if they choose to go to college upon reentry, how many digital skills will they need to master so as to succeed? The failure of the prison system to address the question of digital literacy cuts formerly incarcerated people off from qualifying for a wide array of jobs. We therefore decided to design a course which would teach incarcerated people digital skills. The challenge is exciting; doing digital work under prison-specific constraints is appealing because it pushes against restrictions largely justified on the grounds of security concerns and lack of funds, but which, we are convinced, can be got around when the benefits of acquiring digital skills so far outweigh the inconvenience of building the infrastructure needed for teaching these.

We started to imagine a course which would teach students to code, program and design. We did not want to design a vocational course that will teach students only the skills big corporations demand, for this would be too much like turning them into cogs in a machine. We determined that students would learn transferable marketable skills at the same time that they learned ways to be critical of the skills they learned. They could have agency, and learn, us, what they choose.

Digital Humanities is an interdisciplinary field, with room for everyone. It is radical because it is fundamentally collaborative, destabilizing traditional academic and professional hierarchies and modes of thought. It extends its scope to areas beyond the academy, including the business world. Practitioners in the field of digital humanities are aware that their work can be exclusive of people without expensive computers and broadband, and for the field to be as revolutionary as it wants to be, it needs to include the voices of people from all backgrounds.

Digital humanists create maps and websites and archives and apps and games and projects usually not for commercial gain but in pursuit of knowledge and for the common good. They take historical records and look at them again in another way and come up with very different narratives from the ones that were taught in school. The following words of Miriam Posner, Assistant Professor of Digital Humanities at University of California Los Angeles, conjure up some of the potential of DH:

The great value of teaching DH to undergrads, I have come to believe, is...showing them how provisional, relative, and profoundly ideological is the world being constructed all around us with data. It is an opportunity to show them that our most apparently universal categories—man/woman, black/white—are not inevitable, but the result of very specific power arrangements. Data visualizations, maps, and spreadsheets look terrifyingly authoritative to a nineteen-year-old—and to us, too. One great value of rigorous critical inquiry is that you can help people see how this was all constructed, and to what ideological ends (Miriam Posner, "What's Next: The Radical, Unrealized Potential of Digital Humanities," DH Debates 2016: http://dhdebates.gc.cuny.edu/debates/text/54).

The readings we have selected for this course explore digital humanities theories and methods of inquiry. The tutorials will teach you how to use digital tools to build projects of your own. The selected digital humanities projects in the resources section of this site are examples of work we admire so that you can see some things other people have done.

First and foremost, this course seeks to embrace human dignity as a normative value, as the Vera Institute of Justice so eloquently describes one of its educational goals (see its Reimagining Prison Report included in the Readings section of this site). This pilot course is a first experimental attempt to do digital humanities work in prison. As participants in this course we invite you to suggest improvements and changes in course design as we go along, for this course belongs to you.
